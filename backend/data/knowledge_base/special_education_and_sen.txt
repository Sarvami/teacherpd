Supporting Students with Special Educational Needs

Every classroom includes students with a range of learning differences. Effective teachers understand that these differences are not deficits — they are variations in how people learn, process information, and demonstrate understanding.

---

Understanding the Spectrum of Learning Differences

Common learning differences teachers encounter:

Dyslexia:
- Difficulty with phonological processing, decoding, and spelling
- Does NOT affect intelligence or oral comprehension
- Strategies: multisensory reading instruction, text-to-speech tools, extended time, coloured overlays, dyslexia-friendly fonts

Dyscalculia:
- Difficulty with number sense, arithmetic, and mathematical reasoning
- Strategies: concrete manipulatives, number lines, visual representations, breaking problems into steps

ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder):
- Difficulty with sustained attention, impulse control, and/or hyperactivity
- Strategies: frequent movement breaks, chunked tasks, visual timers, preferential seating, clear and brief instructions, fidget tools

Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC):
- Differences in social communication, sensory processing, and preference for routine
- Strategies: predictable routines, advance notice of changes, visual schedules, quiet workspaces, explicit social instruction, interest-based learning

Developmental Language Disorder (DLD):
- Persistent difficulty understanding and using spoken language
- Often unidentified — students may appear inattentive or uncooperative
- Strategies: simplified instructions, visual supports, checking comprehension individually, avoiding idioms and complex syntax

---

The Social Model of Disability

The medical model of disability locates the "problem" in the individual. The social model locates the barrier in the environment and systems.

In education, this means asking: "What barriers does our classroom, curriculum, or assessment system create for this student?" rather than "What is wrong with this student?"

This shift in framing leads to more effective and dignified support. Instead of trying to "fix" the student, teachers redesign the environment to remove barriers.

---

Individual Education Plans (IEPs)

An IEP is a legally binding document (in many jurisdictions) that outlines:
- The student's current level of performance
- Specific, measurable learning goals
- Accommodations and modifications
- Support services and personnel
- Review timelines

Teachers' responsibilities regarding IEPs:
- Read and understand every IEP for students in your class
- Implement accommodations consistently (not just during assessments)
- Contribute observations and data to IEP reviews
- Communicate regularly with the SENCO/special education coordinator and parents

Accommodations vs. modifications:
- Accommodations change HOW a student accesses learning (extended time, text-to-speech) without changing WHAT is assessed
- Modifications change WHAT is assessed (reduced content, alternative tasks) — these affect the standard being assessed

---

Co-Teaching Models

When a special education teacher or learning support assistant works alongside the classroom teacher, several co-teaching models are possible:

One teach, one observe: One teacher leads instruction while the other collects data on student learning or behaviour.

One teach, one assist: One teacher leads while the other circulates and supports individual students.

Station teaching: Students rotate between stations, each led by one teacher.

Parallel teaching: The class is split in two; both teachers teach the same content simultaneously to smaller groups.

Alternative teaching: One teacher works with a small group on pre-teaching, re-teaching, or enrichment while the other teaches the larger group.

Team teaching: Both teachers share instruction fluidly, playing to each other's strengths.

The most effective model depends on the lesson goals, student needs, and the relationship between the two teachers. Avoid defaulting always to "one teach, one assist" — it underutilises the second teacher and can stigmatise students who receive support.

Sensory Considerations in the Classroom

Many students — particularly those with autism, ADHD, or sensory processing differences — are significantly affected by the sensory environment.

Visual environment:
- Reduce visual clutter on walls and displays
- Use calm, neutral colours where possible
- Ensure adequate lighting without harsh fluorescent glare

Auditory environment:
- Be aware of background noise levels
- Allow noise-cancelling headphones for students who need them
- Give instructions in a calm, clear voice

Physical environment:
- Offer flexible seating options (standing desks, wobble cushions, floor seating)
- Create a quiet corner or calm-down space
- Allow movement breaks

These adjustments benefit all students, not just those with identified needs — this is the principle of Universal Design for Learning in action.

Trauma-Informed Teaching

Many students have experienced adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) — abuse, neglect, domestic violence, poverty, bereavement, or community violence. Trauma affects brain development, emotional regulation, and learning.

Trauma-informed principles:
- Safety: Create a physically and emotionally safe environment. Predictable routines reduce anxiety.
- Trustworthiness: Be consistent, transparent, and follow through on what you say.
- Choice: Offer students agency wherever possible. Trauma often involves loss of control.
- Collaboration: Work with students, not on them.
- Empowerment: Focus on strengths, not deficits.

Behaviour through a trauma lens:
- A student who shuts down, becomes aggressive, or refuses to work may be in a stress response, not being deliberately defiant.
- Ask "What happened to this student?" rather than "What is wrong with this student?"
- Avoid power struggles — they escalate stress responses and damage relationships.
- Prioritise the relationship above the lesson content. A student who doesn't feel safe cannot learn.
