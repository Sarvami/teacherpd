Instructional Coaching Frameworks

Instructional coaching is a form of job-embedded professional development in which a coach works alongside teachers to improve instructional practice. Unlike evaluative supervision, coaching is non-judgmental and teacher-driven.

The GROW Model (Goal, Reality, Options, Will):
- Goal: What does the teacher want to achieve?
- Reality: What is the current situation? What data do we have?
- Options: What strategies or approaches could address the gap?
- Will: What specific action will the teacher take, and by when?

The GROW model is widely used in coaching conversations because it keeps the teacher in the driver's seat. The coach asks questions rather than giving answers.

Reflective Questioning Techniques

Powerful coaching questions open up thinking rather than close it down.

Types of reflective questions:
- Clarifying: "Can you say more about what you mean by that?"
- Probing: "What evidence do you have that students understood the concept?"
- Reframing: "If a student who struggled today came back tomorrow, what would you want them to experience differently?"
- Goal-setting: "What's one small change you could try in the next lesson?"
- Celebrating: "What went well today, and why do you think it worked?"

Avoid leading questions that embed the coach's preferred answer ("Don't you think you should have...?").

Lesson Study

Lesson study is a collaborative professional learning process originating in Japan. A small group of teachers:
1. Collaboratively plan a "research lesson" focused on a specific learning challenge.
2. One teacher teaches the lesson while others observe student learning (not teacher performance).
3. The group debriefs, focusing on evidence of student understanding.
4. The lesson is revised and optionally re-taught.

Lesson study builds shared professional knowledge and shifts the focus from teacher performance to student learning.

Cognitive Coaching

Cognitive coaching (Costa & Garmston) aims to develop the self-directedness of teachers. The coach's role is to mediate thinking, not to transfer knowledge.

The five states of mind that cognitive coaching develops:
1. Efficacy: Belief in one's capacity to make a difference.
2. Flexibility: Willingness to consider multiple perspectives.
3. Craftsmanship: Commitment to continuous improvement.
4. Consciousness: Awareness of one's own thinking and behaviour.
5. Interdependence: Valuing collaboration and community.

Data-Informed Coaching

Effective coaching is grounded in evidence. Types of data used in coaching:
- Student work samples: Analysing student writing, problem-solving, or projects together reveals patterns in understanding and misconceptions.
- Observation data: Scripted notes from classroom observations (e.g., tracking which students are called on, time on task) provide objective starting points for conversation.
- Assessment data: Formative and summative results help identify which students need additional support and which instructional approaches are working.
- Teacher self-assessment: Surveys or reflection tools help teachers identify their own priorities for growth.

The coaching conversation should always return to the question: "What impact did this have on student learning?"
