Early Years and Primary Education

The early years of schooling (ages 3–8) are among the most consequential in a child's development. Brain development is rapid, foundational skills are established, and dispositions toward learning are formed. Teaching in this phase requires deep knowledge of child development alongside strong pedagogical skill.

---

Play-Based Learning

Play is the primary mode of learning for young children. Research consistently shows that high-quality play-based learning develops:
- Language and communication
- Social and emotional skills
- Problem-solving and creativity
- Early mathematical and scientific thinking
- Self-regulation and executive function

Types of play:
- Free play: Child-initiated, open-ended, unstructured
- Guided play: Child-initiated but with teacher involvement to extend thinking
- Structured play: Teacher-designed activities with specific learning goals

The teacher's role in play-based learning:
- Observe: What is the child doing, saying, and thinking?
- Extend: Ask open questions that deepen thinking ("I wonder what would happen if...")
- Provide: Offer resources, vocabulary, and provocations that enrich play
- Document: Record observations to inform planning and share with families

Oral Language Development

Oral language is the foundation of literacy. Children who enter school with strong oral language skills are significantly more likely to become proficient readers and writers.

Strategies to develop oral language:
- Rich conversation: Talk with children, not at them. Ask genuine questions and listen to the answers.
- Shared reading: Reading aloud daily, stopping to discuss vocabulary, predictions, and connections
- Storytelling and retelling: Children retell stories using props, pictures, or puppets
- Show and tell: Children share objects and experiences from home, building vocabulary and confidence
- Dramatic play: Role-play develops narrative language, vocabulary, and perspective-taking

Vocabulary gap: Research shows significant differences in vocabulary size between children from different socioeconomic backgrounds by age 3. Schools can partially address this gap through rich language environments, but early intervention is most effective.

Early Reading: The Science of Reading

The "reading wars" debate between phonics and whole-language approaches has been largely resolved by research. The Simple View of Reading (Gough & Tunmer) shows that reading comprehension = decoding × language comprehension.

Both components are necessary:
- Decoding (phonics): The ability to translate print into sound
- Language comprehension: The ability to understand spoken language

Systematic synthetic phonics:
- Teaching letter-sound correspondences explicitly and in a structured sequence
- Blending sounds to read words (c-a-t → cat)
- Segmenting words to spell them (cat → c-a-t)
- Strong evidence base — recommended by most national reading frameworks

Phonological awareness:
- The ability to hear and manipulate sounds in spoken language
- Includes rhyme, syllable awareness, and phoneme awareness
- Develops before and alongside phonics instruction
- A strong predictor of reading success

Fluency:
- Reading accurately, at an appropriate pace, with expression
- Develops through repeated reading of familiar texts
- Fluency frees up cognitive resources for comprehension

Early Mathematics

Young children develop mathematical understanding through concrete, pictorial, and abstract experiences (the CPA approach, Bruner).

Concrete: Manipulating physical objects (blocks, counters, cubes)
Pictorial: Representing mathematical ideas with pictures and diagrams
Abstract: Using symbols and numbers

Key early mathematics concepts:
- Number sense: Understanding quantity, counting, and the relationships between numbers
- Subitising: Recognising small quantities without counting (seeing 3 dots and knowing it's 3)
- Cardinality: Understanding that the last number counted represents the total quantity
- Composition and decomposition: Understanding that numbers can be broken apart and put together (5 = 3 + 2)
- Spatial reasoning: Understanding shape, space, and position

Common misconceptions:
- Counting without one-to-one correspondence (saying numbers faster than pointing to objects)
- Thinking the last number in a count is just a label, not the total
- Believing that a longer row of objects always has more (conservation of number)

Transitions and School Readiness

Starting school is a significant transition for children and families. School readiness is not just about academic skills — it includes:
- Self-regulation: The ability to manage emotions and behaviour
- Social skills: Taking turns, sharing, listening, resolving conflict
- Independence: Managing personal needs (toileting, dressing, eating)
- Communication: Expressing needs and understanding instructions

Teachers can support transitions by:
- Visiting children in their prior setting (preschool, home)
- Inviting families into the classroom before school starts
- Creating predictable routines that help children feel safe
- Building relationships with families as partners in the child's learning
- Being patient — settling in takes weeks, not days

Observation and Documentation in Early Years

In early years settings, observation is the primary assessment tool. Effective observation:
- Is specific and descriptive, not evaluative ("Aisha sorted the blocks by colour and then by size" not "Aisha is good at maths")
- Captures what the child can do, not what they can't
- Includes the child's own words and explanations
- Is shared with families and used to plan next steps

Learning stories (Carr & Lee): A narrative approach to documentation that captures a child's learning journey in context. Learning stories are written in the second person ("You were building a tower...") and shared with the child and family.

Portfolio assessment: Collections of work samples, photographs, and observations that show growth over time. Portfolios are more meaningful than single-point assessments for young children whose development is rapid and uneven.
