Literacy Across the Curriculum

Literacy is not only the responsibility of English teachers. Every subject has its own disciplinary literacy — the reading, writing, and communication practices specific to that field. Teaching students to read and write like historians, scientists, mathematicians, and artists is a core part of subject teaching.

---

Disciplinary Literacy

Each discipline has characteristic text types, vocabulary, and ways of constructing knowledge:

Science:
- Lab reports, scientific articles, data tables, graphs
- Passive voice, hedging language ("the results suggest...")
- Precision and operational definitions

History:
- Primary sources, secondary analyses, timelines, historiographical essays
- Sourcing (who wrote this, when, and why?), corroboration, contextualisation
- Argument supported by evidence, acknowledgement of counterarguments

Mathematics:
- Word problems, proofs, worked examples
- Precise technical vocabulary (e.g., "factor" means something specific)
- Reading left-to-right AND right-to-left (equations), reading diagrams

The Arts:
- Artist statements, critical reviews, programme notes
- Evaluative and interpretive language
- Connecting personal response to technical analysis

Vocabulary Instruction

Vocabulary knowledge is one of the strongest predictors of reading comprehension and academic achievement. Three tiers of vocabulary (Beck, McKeown & Kucan):

Tier 1: Everyday words (table, run, happy) — rarely need explicit instruction
Tier 2: Academic vocabulary used across subjects (analyse, significant, contrast, evidence) — high instructional priority
Tier 3: Subject-specific technical terms (mitosis, isosceles, impressionism) — taught within the subject

Effective vocabulary instruction:
- Introduce words in context, not as isolated definitions
- Use multiple exposures across different contexts
- Encourage students to use new words in speaking and writing
- Word walls, vocabulary notebooks, and semantic mapping support retention
- Avoid asking students to copy dictionary definitions — this rarely leads to deep understanding

Reading Comprehension Strategies

Explicit comprehension strategy instruction improves reading outcomes, particularly for struggling readers.

Key strategies:
- Activating prior knowledge: Before reading, ask "What do you already know about this topic?"
- Predicting: "What do you think will happen next? What clues in the text support your prediction?"
- Visualising: "Draw or describe the scene/process in your mind"
- Questioning: Students generate their own questions as they read
- Clarifying: Identifying and resolving confusion ("I don't understand this word/sentence — what can I do?")
- Summarising: Identifying the main idea and key supporting details
- Making connections: Text-to-self, text-to-text, text-to-world

Reciprocal teaching (Palincsar & Brown) combines predicting, questioning, clarifying, and summarising in a structured peer-led format that has strong research support.

Writing to Learn

Writing is not only a product — it is a tool for thinking. Writing-to-learn activities help students process and consolidate understanding.

Low-stakes writing activities:
- Exit tickets: "Write one thing you learned today and one question you still have"
- Admit slips: "Write what you remember from last lesson before we begin"
- Quick writes: 3–5 minutes of uninterrupted writing on a prompt
- Learning logs or journals: Regular reflective writing about the learning process
- Concept maps: Visual representations of relationships between ideas

These activities are not graded for correctness — they are thinking tools. The teacher reads them to gauge understanding and plan next steps.

Academic Writing Development

Moving students from informal to formal academic writing requires explicit instruction in:

Structure:
- Paragraph structure (topic sentence, evidence, explanation, link)
- Essay structure (introduction with thesis, body paragraphs, conclusion)
- Discipline-specific structures (lab report, historical essay, literary analysis)

Language features:
- Formal register (avoiding contractions, colloquialisms)
- Hedging and qualifying language ("This suggests..." rather than "This proves...")
- Cohesive devices (however, therefore, in contrast, as a result)
- Nominalisation (turning verbs into nouns: "investigate" → "investigation")

Feedback on writing:
- Focus feedback on one or two priorities per draft, not everything at once
- Use a rubric so students know what is being assessed
- Peer feedback with structured protocols is as effective as teacher feedback for many aspects of writing
- Require students to respond to feedback in a revision — otherwise feedback is often ignored

Supporting English Language Learners in Literacy

ELL students face the dual challenge of learning content and language simultaneously.

Strategies:
- Pre-teach key vocabulary before reading
- Provide bilingual glossaries and translated key texts where possible
- Use graphic organisers to scaffold reading and writing tasks
- Allow students to draft in their home language first, then translate
- Pair ELL students with bilingual peers for discussion tasks
- Celebrate multilingualism: encourage students to make connections between English and their home language

BICS vs. CALP (Cummins):
- Basic Interpersonal Communication Skills (BICS): Conversational fluency, typically acquired in 1–2 years
- Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency (CALP): Academic language needed for school success, typically takes 5–7 years to develop

A student who speaks fluent conversational English may still struggle significantly with academic texts. Teachers should not assume conversational fluency means academic readiness.
